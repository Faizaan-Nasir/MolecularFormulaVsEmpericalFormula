Molecular Formula Versus Emperical Formula

What we need to know in this module:

The meaning of molecular formula is a chemical formula that gives the total number of atoms of each element in each molecule of a substance. We have brushed through this term a few times in the past and are pretty familiar with it by now. The sum of mass of all atoms in the molecule is known as the molecular mass of that compound.

A formula giving the proportions of the elements present in a compound but not the actual numbers or arrangement of atoms is called the emperical formula. This is a new term that we will learn in grade 11 (if you haven't already). A compound with molecular formula C6H12O6, the emperical formula would be the molecular formula divided by the LCM of all the number of atoms. Thus the emperical formula is CH2O. The mass of this formula is known as the emperical formula mass.

The purpose of this module:

Now that we know the meanings of the term, the fields in this module would begin making sense. You may enter the emperical formula in terms of Elements, and the respective number of atoms of that element in it's emperical formula. You must remember not to put the whole emperical formula in the same text block. Taking our example of CH2O, the number of atoms of Carbon is 1, you may now put the compound 'C' in the field E1 and 1 in No. field following the E1 field. You may press the demo button to figure out how to fill the respective fields for our example CH2O.

Please note that as of now, the webpage only works with the atomic masses of the first 20 elements: Hydrogen to Calcium. Any element that is entered into the fields below and has an atomic number more than 20 will cause the webpage to throw an error. Incase the compound only has 2 elements, leave the last two boxes of the emperical formula field empty.
